The right type depends on your goals. Term life insurance is typically more affordable and is ideal if you need coverage for a defined period — for example, while you have a mortgage or young children. Whole Life and Universal Life are permanent policies that also build cash value over time and can be used as part of an estate or tax planning strategy. Our advisors will walk you through the tradeoffs during a free consultation.

The Canadian Super Visa allows parents and grandparents of Canadian citizens or permanent residents to visit Canada for up to two years at a time. A mandatory condition of the Super Visa application is proof of Canadian private medical insurance with a minimum of $100,000 in coverage for at least one year. We specialize in Super Visa-compliant policies and can issue same-day proof of insurance letters required for your application.

Most disability insurance policies replace between 60–85% of your pre-disability earned income, up to a monthly benefit cap set by the insurer. A general rule of thumb is to target enough monthly benefit to cover your essential living expenses — mortgage or rent, utilities, food, and debt payments. We will review your income, existing group coverage through your employer, and any government benefits (CPP disability) to determine the appropriate top-up amount.

Yes. Group benefit plans are available for businesses with as few as two employees, and many carriers offer flexible, cost-effective options designed specifically for small businesses. Additionally, employer premiums paid toward group benefits are generally a deductible business expense, making them tax-efficient for both the employer and the employee. We will design a plan that fits your headcount and budget.

Critical illness insurance pays a one-time, lump-sum tax-free benefit upon diagnosis of a covered condition (e.g., cancer, heart attack, stroke), regardless of whether you are still able to work. Disability insurance pays a monthly income replacement benefit only if you cannot work due to illness or injury. Many Canadians carry both policies together — critical illness covers immediate extraordinary expenses, while disability provides ongoing monthly income replacement during recovery.
